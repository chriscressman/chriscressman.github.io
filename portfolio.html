Architecting a Search Feature

This presentation, delivered to the Lanacaster Elastic User Group this past August, demonstrates my ability to learn quickly and create useful abstractions for teaching. The project, conducted in my free time, required me to learn nine Ruby libraries which provide Elasticsearch integrations with Ruby and Rails. While these libraries provide excellent API documentation, I saw an opportunity to provide a higher-level explanation of how to effectively design a search feature using these tools. In the presentation, I provide an abstract procedure for doing so, and I back it up with concrete examples. To provide the examples, I wrote the model layer and Elasticsearch integrations for an example Ruby on Rails application. I later made the source for this application available as well.

Order Pricing Diagrams

Workarea’s architect and lead engineer told me, “I don’t know how you’re going to explain pricing”, referring to the complexity of the topic. To manage this complexity, I created many diagrams. These diagrams worked backward from the user interface to explain a particular price, because this is the task an engineer is most commonly asked to perform regarding pricing. The diagrams deconstruct the UI into pricing totals API calls, which are each derived by summing price adjustments, which are themselves derived from pricing calculators. By reducing pricing to these three concepts, I made pricing more accessible to Workarea engineers.

Baseball Score Sheet

I played a lot of sports growing up, but as an adult I had the pleasure of being on a baseball team with my two younger brothers, and my dad was the coach. Having been removed from sports for many years, I wasn’t a very good player, but I had a lot of fun keeping score at each game. However, the store-bought score sheets we used were a little too “busy” for me. So, I created my own–in HTML and CSS of course!

Baseball Score Sheet

While a nice score sheet indeed, I include this here mostly to bring up my background in organized sports. Team sports, particularly demanding year-round sports, likely played a large role in shaping who I am today. I attribute many import life and business lessons to my experiences on these teams, such as:

  • Determination: “showing up” (literally and figuratively) every day, even when it’s hard or you don’t want to
  • Rapid learning and skill development: drills, repetition, pushing beyond your comfort zone
  • Reflection: learning from your mistakes; seeing them as opportunities to improve
  • Mission: sharing a vision with others who are equally determined; being a part of something larger than yourself
  • Achievement: trusting the process; watching small gains compound into great achievements
